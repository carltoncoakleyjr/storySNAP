Feeding millions, but falling short

SNAP benefits are back, but New Yorkers face growing food insecurity amid rising costs

By: Carlton Coakley Jr | Nov. 28, 2025

The 43-day federal shutdown is over, but New York City's food insecurity crisis is far from resolved. Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP) benefits-the food lifeline for nearly one in five New Yorkers, have been restored, with November payments issued and December scheduled as normal. But a spending bill passed eariler this year will tighten eligibility in 2026, and city officials warn that more than 200,000 New Yorkers could lose their benefits entirely.

The numbers put the stakes in sharp relief. USDA data shows that SNAP continues to serve roughly 41 million people nationwide each month, yet average benefits have steadily declined since 2021. For New Yorkers, the national trend intersects with an unforgiving local cost of living, leaving SNAP households vulnerable.

Locally, the NYC City Council's 2024 Emergency Food Insecurity dataset paints a stark picture. 23% of residents live below the federal poverty line, nearly double the national rate, and half of all households earns less than the city's true cost of living. Even with SNAP restored, families face a system stretched to its limit, navigating uncertainty about whether their next payment will cover essentials or whether they will qualify at all under new rules.

Some communities bear the brunt of the crisis. Food insecurity ranges from 5% to 36% across NYC's neighborhoods with East Wiliamsburg (Brooklyn), West Farms (Bronx), Belmont (Bronx), Highbridge (Bronx), and Mott Haven (Bronx) topping the list. These neighbourhoods also record high SNAP participation, meaning any disruption would immediately ripple through households already struggling to make ends meet.

Emergency food providers offers a window into the lived reality. Soup kitchens and food pantries report sharp surges in visitors, while school cafeterias have run out of leftovers as parents rely on school meals to fill the gaps at home. While the city maintains 511 emergency food sites, access is uneven, leaving South Brooklyn, Queens, and Staten Island particularly underserved.

Gregory Boroff, Chief External Relations Officer at City Harvest, a food rescue organization that aids millions of New Yorkers with food assistance, said the trend aligns with what their teams are seeing across the city. "For low-income New Yorkers, SNAP is the first line of defense," he said. "When that safety net fluctuates, emergency food programs get hit hard. We start to see demand spike almost immediately."

With policy shifts on the rise in 2026, these gaps threaten to widen. For families in high-need neighborhoods, the question is no longer hypothetical. How will they continue to stretch every dollar, every meal, and every day?
